
# India Hospital Chains FY25 Benchmarking — Case Study

## Interview-Ready Summary

This case study benchmarks the four largest listed Indian hospital chains — Apollo Hospitals, Fortis Healthcare, Max Healthcare, and Narayana Health — on FY25 margin and operational performance to test a specific hypothesis: that payer mix (cash vs. insurance vs. government scheme) is the primary driver of margin differences across the sector. The analysis finds the opposite — **occupancy and case-mix/cost-structure discipline explain the margin spread far better than payer mix does**: Max Healthcare posts both the highest occupancy (75%) and the highest hospital-segment EBITDA margin (26.8%), while Apollo and Fortis run nearly identical occupancy (68% vs. 69%) yet diverge by 370 basis points on margin, and Narayana Health achieves a respectable 23.3% margin despite the lowest occupancy in the set — evidence that a low-cost, standardized operating model can substitute for both occupancy and premium payer mix. The complication layered on top is structural: insurance-driven hospital revenue is growing 20%+ annually and increasingly rewards complex case mix, while government-scheme reimbursement (Ayushman Bharat/PM-JAY) is simultaneously scaling in enrollment but seeing private hospitals quietly disengage — monthly empanelments collapsed from ~316 to ~111 between 2024 and early 2025 — because package rates don't cover costs for tertiary care. The recommendation is therefore to prioritize occupancy/utilization and cost-structure discipline as the primary margin levers, treat case-mix upgrades as margin-accretive only when matched by cost control, and use government-scheme participation selectively (tier-2/3 volume building) rather than as a core profitability strategy in flagship urban hospitals. Payer-mix data is the least standardized disclosure in the sector, so every payer-mix figure in this analysis is explicitly flagged as confirmed (company-disclosed) or estimated (analyst/brokerage-derived, or extrapolated from an adjacent period).

## How figures are flagged

- **Confirmed**: Revenue, EBITDA, EBITDA margin, occupancy (Apollo/Fortis/Max), ARPOB, and ARPOB growth all come directly from company FY25 results releases or investor decks.
- **Estimated (flagged yellow in the Excel, flagged with asterisks/notes in the deck)**: Apollo's full payer-mix split (last clean disclosure was FY24, used as an FY25 proxy), Max's corporate/institutional revenue share (extrapolated backward from an FY26 disclosure), and Narayana Health's occupancy rate (not precisely disclosed by the company; figure is a brokerage estimate based on management commentary).
- Payer mix specifically is the least standardized disclosure across all four chains — each company uses different categories and disclosure frequency — so cross-chain payer-mix comparisons should be read as directional, not as a precise reconciled dataset. This is called out explicitly on the appendix slide and in the Excel's Sources & Notes tab.

## Suggested talking points for the interview

1. **Lead with the finding, not the data**: occupancy and cost discipline beat payer mix as the explanatory variable — this is a more interesting (and more defensible) insight than "insurance is growing so margins are under pressure."
2. **Use Fortis vs. Apollo as the key proof point** — nearly identical occupancy, meaningfully different margin — to show the hypothesis test actually discriminated between competing explanations rather than confirming the obvious.
3. **Be ready to defend the estimate flags** — an interviewer may push on the Narayana occupancy estimate or the Apollo FY24-as-FY25-proxy choice; the honest answer is that full payer-mix and occupancy disclosure is inconsistent across Indian listed hospital chains, which is itself a useful observation about the sector's reporting maturity.
